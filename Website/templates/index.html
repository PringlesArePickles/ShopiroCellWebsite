What are cells?

Cells are the simplest life forms, with the most basic cell being called a prokaryote.

Cells evolved from bacteria eating one another

A bacteria eats another bacteria, and if they benefit eachother, the ingested bacteria gets to stay.

Eventually the bacteria (cell) engulfed enough bacteria to look like the cells we see today.

This theory is called Endosymbiosis, created by Lynn Margulis in 2001

Endosymbiotic theory led to the creation of organelles.

Organelles to a cell are like organs to humans.

History of Cell Discovery

Robert Hooke (1665):

First to observe and name "cells" after looking at cork under a microscope. Saw empty "cell" walls in plant material.

Antonie van Leeuwenhoek (1674):

Discovered "animalcules" (single-celled organisms) by observing pond water under a microscope. First to observe living cells. First to observe living cells.

Matthias Schleiden (1838):
Proposed that all plants are made of cells.
Theodor Schwann (1839):
Proposed that all animals are made of cells. Rudolf Virchow (1855): Concluded that all cells come from pre-existing cells.
Cell Theory:

1. All living things are made of cells (Schleiden & Schwann).

2. The cell is the basic unit of structure and function in living organisms.

3. All cells arise from preexisting cells (Virchow).

4. All cells contain genetically inherited material(DNA/RNA)

Cell Structure and Organelles

Cell Membrane

Surrounds the cell. Regulates passage of materials (oxygen, nutrients, waste) in/out of the cell. See how a cell membrane works.

Cell Wall

Plant cells only. Surrounds the cell membrane. Provides protection and support for the cell and protects it.

Cytosol

Inside the cell membrane but outside the nucleus. A gel-like substance that provides support for organelles and helps move materials within the cell. Cytosol + organelles = cytoplasm.

Nucleus

In the center of animal cells (not in the center of plant cells). Directs cell activity like the brain. Contains chromatin made of DNA and proteins.

Nuclear Membrane

Surrounds the nucleus. Regulates passage of materials in and out through nuclear pores, letting ribosomes out but not DNA.

Nucleolus

Located inside the nucleus. Produces ribosomes and is made of RNA and proteins.

Ribosomes

Found in the cytoplasm or attached to the rough ER. They are protein factories – the site of protein synthesis, made of RNA and protein.

Endoplasmic Reticulum

Located in the cytoplasm and bound to the nuclear envelope. It comes in two forms: Smooth and Rough.

Smooth ER

Synthesizes/makes lipids, stores enzymes or calcium ions, and can break down some poisons. Parts of it can repair the cell membrane.

Rough ER

Modifies/edits proteins made by ribosomes; ribosomes attach to it.

Golgi Body

In the cytoplasm. A stack of flattened sacs that modify, package, and distribute substances made by the ER. Looks like a stack of pita packets. Scientists don’t know exactly how it works.

Lysosome

Found in the cytoplasm. Filled with digestive enzymes and acid, it breaks down food, foreign particles, and old organelles. Produced by the RER and Golgi.

Vacuole

In animal cells, it’s found in the cytoplasm; in plant cells, it is usually a large central vacuole that stores water, nutrients, and wastes, and helps regulate turgor.

Peroxisomes

Small sacs in the cytoplasm containing enzymes that detoxify alcohol and break down hydrogen peroxide (H2O2), a toxic byproduct of cellular reactions.

Mitochondria

Found in the cytoplasm. Known as the powerhouse of the cell, it is the site of cellular respiration where sugars are converted to ATP. It has a double membrane and its own DNA due to endosymbiosis.

Chloroplast

Found only in plant cells. The site of photosynthesis, it uses light energy to make sugars. It has a double membrane, contains chlorophyll, its own DNA, and divides independently.

Microtubule Organizing Center

Located in the cytoplasm near the nucleus. It is where microtubules are first formed and helps in forming flagella, cilia, and spindle fibers during cell division. In animal cells, it contains centrioles.

Centrioles

Assist in the formation of spindle fibers during cell division and in forming eukaryotic flagella and cilia.

Cytoskeleton

The structural framework that gives the cell its shape. It provides pathways for proteins and vesicles to move within the cell.

Cytoskeleton 1 (Microtubules)

Hollow tubulin protein tubes that support and shape the cell. They aid in moving organelles and can form centrioles, cilia, and flagella.

Cytoskeleton 2 (Microfilaments)

Long, thin actin protein fibers that aid in cytoplasmic streaming and are specialized for contraction, making them abundant in muscle cells.

Cytoskeleton 3 (Intermediate Filaments)

Fibrous, rope-like proteins that reinforce the cytoskeleton and help anchor some organelles.

Flagella

Tail-like structures that allow locomotion in certain single-celled organisms (e.g., sperm and protists), functioning like propellers.

Cilia

Hair-like structures that move in unison to push materials over the cell surface in multicellular organisms.

Plant vs. Animal

Because of Endosymbiotic Theory, cells have evolved into different categories, most commonly plant and animal, each with different characteristics and functions.

Plant Animal
Have a cell wall Do not have a cell wall
Has a large, central vacuole Has a vacuole, but is smaller and off to the side
Has a smaller nucleus off to the side Has a larger, more central nucleus
Contains no centrioles inside the MTOC Contains centrioles inside the MTOC
